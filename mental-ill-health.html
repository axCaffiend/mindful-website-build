What is Mental Ill Health?

When our mental health is poor, this is called mental ill health.
This can range from feeling a 'bit down', to common disorders such as anxiety and depression, to more severe and less common conditions such as bipolar disorder or schizophrenia.

It's normal for mental health to change...

Most people's mental health will not just be continuously good. Usually, it will rise and fall depending on pressures and/or experiences in their life - a person may feel in good mental health generally, but also experience highs and lows as their mental health fluctuates in response to their environment or situation.

People under a lot of pressure may experience stress, which in itself is not a mental health condition, but can lead to anxiety and depression.

Did you know...

Two-thirds of students have either been diagnosed with a long-term mental health condition, are experiencinga short-term mental health condition or think they may have a condition that has not been diagnosed.

Types of Mental Ill Health

Here are some examples of conditions that can affect us when we have poor mental health.

Anxiety

It's natural to feel anxious at times, but if this is persistent over long periods of time, people may find it difficult to deal with everyday life where feelings of tension and fear prevent them from completing everyday tasks. In some cases, people may suffer panic attacks or phobias.

Obsessive Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

OCD is a form of anxiety where people have recurrent, compulsive thoughts which they may feel forced to act on (e.g. a fear of contamination resulting in excessive hand washing).

Depression

Sometimes described as mild, moderate or severe, symptoms of depression can include feelings of deep sadness that can last for weeks or months. In severe cases, people may view life as not worth living, leading to suicidal behaviour.

Bipolar Disorder

Also referred to as manic depression. These terms describe a condition where a person's mood swings between episodes of extreme low mood and depressive symptoms to being high or elated.

Psychosis and schizophrenia

When a person appears to lose touch with reality.
This might involve seeing or hearing things that other people cannot see or hear (hallucinations) and believing things that are not actually true (delusions). It may also involve confused (disordered) thinking and speaking.

Eating Disorders and addictons

Eating disorders (e.g. anorexia and bulimia) and drug and alcohol addictions can also be associated with mental health problems.
